Company Origins & Heritage

Forged in the
Vacuum of Space.

Space is the ultimate filter for bad engineering. You cannot send a mechanic to the Moon. Our founder, Jan, built the foundation of Passengers5719 by solving hardware problems for the harshest environments off-world, validated directly by NASA.

2022 — 2023
NASA Open Innovation System Design

Mastering the Regolith:
The Particle Partition Challenge

Lunar regolith is not like Earth dust. It is hyper-abrasive, electromagnetically charged, and destroys conventional moving parts within hours. NASA launched the Particle Partition Challenge (Project 157150) to find ways to protect deep-space mechanisms from this destructive environment.

Jan developed a winning architecture to manage and mitigate particulate ingress. Winning this challenge laid the engineering groundwork for our company: if you want to build a mechanism that survives, you must rethink how physical components interact with the surface they touch. Zero maintenance isn't a luxury in space—it is a physical law.

Late 2024
Genesis Strategic Thesis

The Foundation of
Passengers5719

Looking back at Earth, the stagnation in terrestrial automotive engineering became glaring. Automakers were building heavier EVs with the exact same vulnerable, passive drivetrains used since the 1900s. Meanwhile, robotics was achieving true embodied intelligence.

Passengers5719 was established to force the convergence. We took the mandate of aerospace engineering—absolute reliability, minimized points of failure, and structural intelligence—and applied it to the wheel. The concept of the 54-spoke, sentient, self-propelling wheel was born.

Early 2026
NASA Center of Excellence Phase 1 Winner

Designing the Lunar Terrain Vehicle:
Rock & Roll with NASA

NASA required a new wheel design for the upcoming Lunar Terrain Vehicle (LTV)—a crewed and uncrewed rover that will navigate the lunar South Pole. Rubber tires fail in cryogenic cold; rigid wheels get stuck.

Applying the Passengers5719 philosophy, Jan designed a highly compliant, non-pneumatic wheel architecture. The geometry allowed the wheel to absorb impact, conform to the terrain, and generate traction without complex internal drivetrains. The design was selected as a Phase 1 Winner by NASA's Center of Excellence for Collaborative Innovation.

April 2026
Hardware Validation Bammental to Houston

From Digital to Physical:
Shipping to NASA JSC

Simulations do not move payloads. To prove the efficacy of the compliant spoke architecture, Jan fabricated an 18-inch physical prototype of the wheel. Moving from digital CAD to a tangible, load-bearing object required intense material sourcing and precision assembly.

In April 2026, the prototype was shipped from our facilities in Germany directly to NASA's Johnson Space Center in Houston, Texas. NASA engineers evaluated the hardware physically. This transition from concept to tangible, aerospace-validated hardware represents our definitive proof of principle.
