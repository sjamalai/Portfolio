The Challenge

NASA's Rock & Roll with NASA challenge — hosted through HeroX — invited engineers worldwide to design and physically fabricate an 18-inch airless wheel capable of operating across the rock-strewn, dust-laden terrain of planetary and lunar surfaces. Unlike pneumatic tyres, airless wheels cannot fail from puncture or pressure loss, making them essential for long-duration rover missions where maintenance is impossible.

The winning design needed to demonstrate structural integrity under load, terrain compliance across irregular rock geometry, and the manufacturing precision required for aerospace-grade hardware — all in a single physical submission shipped directly to NASA Johnson Space Center for hands-on evaluation.

Design Approach
"An airless wheel is a structural problem disguised as a tyre problem — the geometry must flex where rubber would, and stand firm where the rim must."

The design process began with the fundamental mechanics of airless tyre behaviour: load must be transferred from the hub to the contact patch through a compliant intermediate structure that deflects under local obstacle loads while maintaining a stable overall radius. This rules out rigid spoke geometries and demands a lattice or continuous shell approach tuned to the correct stiffness-to-mass ratio.

The spoke and tread geometry was developed parametrically in SolidWorks, with structural analysis run across simulated rock-contact load cases to validate deflection and stress margins before committing to fabrication. The physical prototype was then built to match the CAD geometry within aerospace-appropriate tolerances — a requirement underscored by the fact that NASA evaluators would be handling and loading the wheel directly.

Physical Submission

The completed 18-inch airless wheel — weighing 10 kg — was shipped via UPS Expedited from Bammental, Germany to NASA Johnson Space Center in Houston, Texas, arriving at Building 420 for evaluation by NASA's challenge review team. The shipment was accompanied by full commercial documentation and a DD Form 1149 material shipment record, as required for non-commercial hardware entering a US government facility.

The journey from a CAD model in Germany to a physical wheel on a NASA evaluator's bench is the most direct validation possible: not a simulation, not a render — a real object, built to the design, crossing an ocean to be judged by the agency that will one day drive wheels like it across the Moon.

Outcome

The submission was recognised as an award-winning entry in the NASA Rock & Roll with NASA Challenge 2025 — Jamal's second NASA challenge win, following the Particulate Partition System award in 2023. Two documentation videos covering the build process and wheel-in-motion testing are available on the project blog.
