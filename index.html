First Principles Thinking

"Don't start with what exists—start with what's physically possible."

Jamal's design philosophy is rooted in first principles thinking: the practice of stripping a problem down to its fundamental truths and rebuilding solutions from the ground up, unconstrained by convention or prior art.


Rather than inheriting assumptions baked into existing designs, Jamal begins every CAD project by asking what the physics actually demands. What forces are at play? What materials behave optimally under those loads? What geometry satisfies the constraints with the least material and the highest performance margin?


This method produced the 85% fuel savings in the counter-flow burner project—not through incremental iteration, but by questioning whether a large burner was ever necessary in the first place. The same discipline drove the NASA particulate partition winning entry: rather than adapting a conventional filter mechanism, Jamal re-derived the separation problem from first-order fluid dynamics and material science principles.

01
Decompose to Fundamentals

Every design begins with a clean-sheet question: what does physics require here? Assumptions inherited from prior designs are treated as hypotheses to be tested, not constraints to be accepted.

02
Geometry as Reasoning

CAD geometry is not decoration—it is a direct expression of engineering logic. Every surface, fillet, and tolerance encodes a decision derived from load paths, manufacturing physics, or assembly intent.

03
Constraint as Creative Force

Tight constraints—miniaturization targets, fuel budgets, aerospace tolerances—are treated as design inputs, not obstacles. Working within extreme limits forces more elegant, higher-order solutions than unconstrained design ever produces.

04
Validate Against Reality

A first-principles solution must close against the real world. Analytical estimates, simulation results, and physical intuition are cross-checked at every stage to ensure the reasoning chain holds before committing to final geometry.
